Outrigger Canoeing

What is Outrigger Canoeing?

An outrigger canoe is a canoe with a lateral float — called an ama — connected by booms known as iakos, providing exceptional stability on open water. The sport has deep Polynesian roots and is central to Hawaiian, Tahitian, and Pacific Island culture. Outrigger canoes are paddled solo (OC-1), double (OC-2), or as a six-person crew (OC-6), making it one of the most versatile and culturally rich paddle sports in the world.

Types of Outrigger Canoes

OC-1 (Solo)

Length: 19'–23'

The solo outrigger canoe, built for racing and fitness paddling. Lightweight and fast, the OC-1 is the choice for competitive solo athletes who want to challenge themselves on the open ocean.

OC-2 (Two-Person)

A two-person outrigger canoe used for training and recreational paddling. Great for building technique and coordination with a partner before stepping into larger crew boats.

OC-6 (Six-Person Crew Boat)

Length: 40'+

The heart of the sport. The OC-6 is a six-person crew boat used in team racing. Paddling an OC-6 is a deeply communal experience — timing, rhythm, and trust between crew members are everything.

Surfski / OC Hybrid

A crossover design built for downwind runs, combining the stability of an outrigger with the speed of a surfski. Popular among experienced paddlers chasing open-ocean swells.

Essential Gear

OC-1 Canoe

Your primary craft for solo outrigger paddling. Available in fiberglass and carbon layups.

$2,000 – $5,000

OC Paddle (Single-Blade, Carbon)

A lightweight single-blade paddle, typically carbon fiber, designed for efficient outrigger strokes.

$150 – $500

PFD (Personal Flotation Device)

A low-profile life vest that allows full range of paddling motion. Required for most races and recommended at all times.

$50 – $150

Hydration Pack

Stay hydrated during long paddles and races. A small deck-mounted or wearable hydration system is essential for open-water sessions.

$20 – $40

Ama Cord / Lashing

Used to secure the ama to the iakos. Proper lashing technique is a fundamental skill every outrigger paddler should learn.

Bailer

A simple scoop for removing water from the hull. Essential for open-ocean paddling where waves regularly wash over the canoe.

Leash

Keeps you tethered to the canoe in case of a huli (capsize). Critical for solo OC-1 paddling in open water.

Getting Started

  • Join a club — OC-6 is the best way to start. You will learn technique, ocean awareness, and the culture of the sport from experienced paddlers.
  • Learn the six paddling positions. Each seat in an OC-6 has a specific role, from the stroker in seat 1 to the steersperson in seat 6.
  • Focus on timing with your crew. Outrigger is about synchronization — matching your stroke to the paddler in front of you is more important than raw power.
  • Most clubs provide boats and gear for beginners. You do not need to buy anything to get started. Just show up ready to paddle.

Who Is It For?

Outrigger canoeing is for team sport lovers, ocean paddlers, and anyone drawn to Polynesian culture and community. OC-6 paddling is highly social — you will train, race, and travel with your crew. For those who prefer solo competition, OC-1 racing offers an intense, rewarding challenge on the open ocean.
